# On Silence

There is a silence that falls over a forest at dusk that is not the absence of sound. It is a listening silence, the kind of silence a deer makes when it freezes at the edge of a clearing, ears cupped toward the trees. Silence like this is itself a sound — the sound of attention sharpening.

We speak of silence as if it were empty, a vacuum. But every silence has a texture. The silence inside a cathedral is thick, stone-cushioned, perfumed with centuries of breath. The silence of an empty apartment in a new city is a thinner silence, flat and uncomfortable, the silence of a stranger's furniture. The silence between two people who understand each other is the best silence of all — warm, deliberate, chosen.

I think often about the silence of libraries. Not the imposed silence of the librarian's finger to her lips, but the collective concentration of two dozen strangers each pursuing a private obsession. You can feel the thought moving through the room like a current under ice.

Musicians understand silence better than the rest of us. A rest is not a gap but a structural note — as consequential as any C# or F. Miles Davis built an entire aesthetic from what he did not play. The spaces between the notes, he said, are where the magic happens. The best conversations follow the same rule.

We are losing our silences. Open-plan offices, earbuds, the constant tutting of a phone on a desk. We have filled every gap in the day with podcasts, with reels, with the sound of other people's thinking. It is not that we hate silence. It is that silence has become strange, a thing we must deliberately seek out rather than something that falls naturally.

I try to carry a silence with me now. Ten minutes in the morning before I touch a screen. Five minutes in the afternoon at a window. A long walk without headphones. It is not meditation. It is something closer to air — something I had forgotten I needed.

The deer at the edge of the clearing knows something about silence we have forgotten. It is not absence. It is attention. It is the moment before you understand.

A friend told me once that her grandmother, deaf from an early age, said she pitied hearing people for never knowing silence. What they mistook for silence, the grandmother said, was merely a softer noise. True silence, she said, is a presence, a room in the house of the mind. I have turned that over many times and I think she was right.

Outside my window a crow is calling. In the pause between its calls I can hear the wind moving in the upper branches. In the pause between the wind I can hear nothing at all, and that nothing is full of everything, and I do not know how to write about it except by saying that I am grateful.
