# Mercury: Three Meanings

The word "Mercury" refers to at least three distinct things that share nothing beyond a name, and confusing them is a common error.

## Mercury the Planet

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in our solar system. It has an orbital period of about 88 Earth days. Its surface is heavily cratered, resembling the Moon, and temperatures swing from roughly -170°C at night to 430°C in the day due to the lack of a substantial atmosphere. NASA's MESSENGER orbiter (2011–2015) and the joint ESA-JAXA BepiColombo mission (launched 2018) are the primary modern explorations.

The planet was known to ancient civilizations but is hard to observe from Earth because it never strays far from the Sun in the sky — it appears at most about 28° from the Sun and is usually visible only at dawn or dusk.

## Mercury the Element

Mercury is a chemical element with symbol Hg and atomic number 80. It is the only metal that is liquid at standard temperature and pressure, a property that gave it the historical name "quicksilver." Its silver sheen and flowing mobility made it a laboratory curiosity for centuries.

Mercury is highly toxic. Exposure damages the nervous system, kidneys, and developing fetuses. The Minamata disease outbreak in Japan, caused by industrial methylmercury contamination of fish, prompted the Minamata Convention on Mercury, a UN treaty signed in 2013 aimed at phasing out mercury use globally.

Common uses include fluorescent lighting (being phased out), dental amalgams, and certain industrial catalysts. Thermometers and barometers historically used mercury but are now largely replaced by digital or alcohol alternatives.

## Mercury the Roman God

Mercury (Latin: Mercurius) was the Roman god of commerce, communication, travelers, boundaries, trickery, and thieves. He was the messenger of the gods and conductor of souls to the underworld. His Greek counterpart is Hermes, from whom most of his iconography derives.

Mercury is traditionally depicted with winged sandals (the talaria), a winged cap (the petasus), and the caduceus — a staff entwined with two serpents. The caduceus is often confused with the Rod of Asclepius (single serpent, no wings), which is the correct symbol of medicine; the conflation is an American quirk.

His festival, the Mercuralia, was celebrated on May 15. Merchants would sprinkle themselves and their goods with water from his sacred spring near the Porta Capena in Rome to request profitable dealings.

## The Shared Name

All three Mercurys descend from the Roman god. The planet was named for him because of its swift apparent motion across the sky; the element for the same reason — mercury flows "swiftly" like the god's messengers. But beyond that shared etymology, the three concepts have nothing in common, and precise writing should always disambiguate.
